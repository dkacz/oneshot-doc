# Style Rules (Shared Across Presets)

Rules apply in both languages unless marked otherwise. The style critic enforces them directly.

## Shared Rules

1. **No long dashes** (`—`, `–`) anywhere in the document. Use a normal spaced hyphen, colon, comma, parentheses, or a new sentence. Ranges use a normal hyphen (2028-2030).
2. **Concept before use.** Every technical term receives a plain-language definition at first use. Method pattern: bold term, simple explanation, concrete example, notation only after that.
3. **Short sentences, active voice.** Keep the subject near the verb. A sentence above about 25 words is a candidate for splitting.
4. **No AI cliches.** Avoid "warto zauważyć", "co istotne", "w dzisiejszym dynamicznym świecie", "it is important to note", "delve", "landscape", and decorative "robust". Avoid summaries that only repeat the preceding paragraph.
5. **No colon-labels as syntax crutches** ("Conclusion: ..."). The label becomes part of the sentence ("The conclusion is that ...") or becomes a heading.
6. **Numbers are consistent.** The same number is written the same way in text, figure, and table. Polish uses a decimal comma; English uses a decimal point. Ranges and units are consistent across the document.
7. **Bridges between sections.** The last sentence of a section opens the next one; the reader never falls into a new section without a transition.
8. **Tone from the contract.** Emotional temperature, register, and stance toward institutions are set in the contract; style does not drift between sections.

## No Metanarration or Defensive Prose (Shared, Hard)

16. **The document speaks about its subject, not about itself.** Metanarration is forbidden ("niniejszy dokument podsumowuje...", "celem tej notatki jest...", "as noted above"), as are references to the production process (contract, production brief, card, revision rounds, interview settlements) and codenames or labels created during production (working codes, internal variant or task names, labels such as X1 or v4), unless the name is established audience nomenclature confirmed in the contract. Preset genre elements (a "Dotyczy:" line, recommendation box, signature) are not metanarration. The content must stand alone: a reader outside the process must not encounter a trace that the document was produced under someone's production arrangements.

17. **No defensive prose.** The document does not argue with comments, pre-empt reviewer objections, or explain itself out of forbidden zones or omissions ("dokument celowo pomija...", "świadomie nie omawiamy...", "despite possible objections...", "this is not a weakness because..."). After a comment round, improve the content; do not add responses to comments into the document. Genre-appropriate explanation of method limits, for example caveats in a research method note or variant costs in a decision memo, is allowed when it speaks about the subject rather than defending the document before the reader.

## English

13. **No compound modifier chains.** Rephrase instead of "category-specific market-anchored haircut"; prefer "haircut anchored to market categories".
14. **Plain verbs over nominalisations.** "We estimate", not "estimation was performed".
15. **One idea per sentence in abstracts and lead boxes.**

## Appendix: Polish-Language Documents

For Polish-language documents:

9. **Avoid Anglicisms** when a natural Polish equivalent exists. An English term may appear once in parentheses at the first use of the Polish equivalent.
10. **Use full Polish diacritics**; no "moze", "wazne".
11. **Newly coined mechanism names** are introduced with "tak zwany" or a definition, not as if they were common concepts.
12. **Use personal forms instead of impersonal chains**: "badanie pokazuje", not "zostało pokazane, że".
